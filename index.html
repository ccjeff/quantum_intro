The Uncertainty Principle, 1927

German physicist Werner Heisenberg introduces the uncertainty principle, which asserts that you can't know everything about a quantum particle at the same time. The more you know about its position, the less you know about its momentum, and vice versa.

Quantum Information Theory, 1970

Notes taken from discussions between Stephen Wiesner and Charlie Bennett (who at the time was still a graduate student at Harvard) contain what may be the first use of the phrase “quantum information theory” and the first suggestion for using entanglement as a communication resource. The notes also describe the principle of superdense coding, eventually published in 1992 by Wiesner and Bennett.

Shor's Factoring Algorithm, 1994

MIT’s Peter Shor shows that it’s possible to factor a number into its primes effeciently on a quantum computer — a problem that takes classical computers “an exponentially long time” to solve for large numbers. His algorithm launches an explosion of theoretical and experimental interest in the field of quantum computing.
